Foreword

This document is part of the SDG Localization project financially supported by the Andalusian Agency for International Development Cooperation (AACID) and implemented by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) in partnership with the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) and the provinces of Maputo and Cabo Delgado.

The document compiles the experiences gained in applying the methodology for localizing the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in Mozambique, used by the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) and the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) in six provinces: Maputo, Cabo Delgado, Sofala, Manica, Niassa and Nampula. This process is fundamental to ensuring that the SDGs are effectively integrated into local policies and actions, thus contributing to the country’s sustainable development.

Each chapter presents concrete examples of how the provinces have implemented the actions necessary for the effective localization of the SDGs in their territories. The aim of the document is to serve as a support guide for the other provinces in the country that are about to start this process.

To draw them up, various manuals, guidelines and methodologies for localizing the SDGs were consulted and adapted to the specific context of Mozambique. This approach ensures that the guidelines are relevant and applicable to the local reality.

Since the introduction of the decentralization package in 2019, the localization of the SDGs has been carried out at provincial level, contributing to their implementation, particularly in planning and budgeting instruments, as well as monitoring the decentralization process itself in the country.

We would like to thank the Andalusian Agency for International Development Cooperation (AACID) for its financial support, as well as the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF) and the provinces involved, whose collaboration was essential to the success of this project.

I. Background

What does localizing the SDGs mean?

The 2030 Agenda, adopted by all United Nations Member States in 2015, provides a shared blueprint for peace and prosperity for people and the planet, both now and in the future. At its heart are the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which constitute an urgent call for joint global aimed at promoting sustainable development in its many dimensions: social, economic, and environmental. It also emphasizes the importance of peace, and institutional effectiveness. The new approach doesn’t just set targets, but also encourages the creation of spaces for dialogue and collaboration, where everyone’s voices can be heard.

Local and regional governments are essential for promoting inclusive sustainable development within their territories and, therefore, are necessary partners in the impementation of the SDGs.

Effective local governance can ensure the inclusion of a diversity of local stakeholders, thereby creating broadbased ownership, commitment and accountability.

An integrated multi-level and multistakeholder approach is needed to promote transformative agendas at the local level.

Strong national commitment to provide adequate legal frameworks and institutionl and financial capacity are required.

The SDGs recognize that the eradication of poverty and other deprivations must be accompanied by strategies that improve health and education, reduce inequalities and stimulate economic growth, while combating climate change and working to preserve our oceans and forests. With the rapid changes the world is facing, especially as we approach 2030, it is becoming increasingly urgent to establish dynamics that promote solidarity and accelerate progress towards the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Effective decision-making requires a strategic and systematic approach to manage various resources, including human, financial, knowledge, and institutional. By doing so, decision-makers can foster and accelerate necessary transformations.1

To meet the objectives of the 2030 Agenda, it is necessary to “localize” the SDGs. This involves everything from creating goals and targets to choosing ways to implement them and using indicators to track and measure success. Localization relates to the ways in which the SDGs can serve as a framework for local development strategy and how local and central governments can support their achievement through grassroots initiatives.

Although the SDGs are global, their realization depends on the ability to make them a reality at the local level. All the SDGs have targets directly related to the responsibilities of local and central governments, especially in relation to their role in providing basic services. For this reason, local and central governments should be at the heart of the 2030 Agenda, and this is the case in Mozambique, as will be explored throughout this guide for Localizing the SDGs through specific cases and activities implemented by the UNDP Decentralization Program in Mozambique.

Locating the SDGs in Mozambique

The Republic of Mozambique is located on the southeast coast of Africa and is administratively divided into 11 provinces, 161 districts, 408 administrative posts, 1132 localities and 65 municipalities.

In terms of extension, the country has an area of 799,380 Km2 of which around 1.65% (13,000 Km2) correspond to the maritime area which is bathed by the Indian Ocean for a length of 2,470 km. The country’s climate is tropical, humid in the north and coastal areas, and dry in the south and inland. It should be noted that Mozambique’s geographical location places the country in a situation of cyclical vulnerability to the effects of Climate Change with devastating consequences.2

As part of the new local governance framework, resulting from the one-off revision of the Constitution of the Republic in 2018, the provinces now have the so-called Organs of Decentralized Provincial Governance (OGDPs) and the State Representation in the Province. The new decentralization framework came into force in the 2018 local elections and, in the case of the provinces, with the 2019 Provincial Assembly elections. The 2018 municipal elections, as well as the presidential, legislative and provincial assembly elections in 2019, were all considered by the African Union, SADC and other international bodies to be free, fair and transparent.

In 2020, Mozambique volunteered to carry out an assessment of the implementation of the Sustainable Development Goals, the Voluntary National Report 2020 (VNR), to share its progress, challenges, and good practices with society. This stance is in line with the principle that has been consolidated in the country, namely transparency, participation, and inclusion in identifying the challenges and solutions that the country must face.

Specifically, this exercise aimed to assess: (a) the process of internalizing and integrating the SDGs into development plans and strategies; (b) progress in implementing the SDGs and good practices and challenges; (c) the capacity to coordinate and monitor the SDGs and the involvement of different actors.3

The National Voluntary Report 2020 concluded, in relation to the Localization of the SDGs, that:

It was necessary to support the provinces in establishing their participation structures at this level, with the involvement of all the relevant players in the implementation of the 2030 Agenda. RNV has helped to activate the participation of the provinces and contributed to the operationalization of the technical committees of the National Reference Group in the provinces. It is anticipated that this will enhance implementation efforts, thereby improving the localization of actions. This approach aims to achieve the objective of leaving no one behind by fostering greater participation from local actors. This process will contribute to training in SDG localization for planners in decentralized and parliamentary bodies and to the development of an SDG localization guide to help local governments integrate the SDGs and cross-cutting themes (gender, climate change, resilience, etc.).

Furthermore, in general terms, regarding the SDGs in Mozambique, the RNV 2020 concluded that a significant proportion of the population does not have adequate knowledge about the SDGs. In this sense, an urgent need was identified to raise awareness, leveraging institutions with access to communities throughout the country. The National SDG Review Group, and the focal points of the National Peer Review Mechanism (APRM), provide important resources and platforms in Mozambique to expand awareness of the SDGs.

The effective implementation of the SDGs requires adequate capacity at local authority level to engage with local communities and other stakeholders. This implies that the capacity of members of the Municipal, Provincial and, in the future, District Assemblies and Members of Parliament be increased to effectively engage with their constituents. The engagement strategy will aim to develop coalitions, mechanisms and forms of participation that can guarantee ample involvement, especially of vulnerable groups and others with low participation, such as people with disabilities, young people, women and the elderly, and different income groups. Creative use of the press and social networks, community radio and traditional forms of disseminating information will be an important tool for reaching a greater proportion of the population. RNV 2020 was an exercise supported by UNDP Mozambique on localizing the SDGs.

The main body responsible for localizing the SDGs in Mozambique is the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF). The localization of the SDGs in the Provinces and Districts is done considering the methodological guidelines of the Central Government in the preparation of planning and budgeting, monitoring and evaluation instruments that ensure the integration of the SDGs.

Given its multi-stakeholder representative nature, in 2017 Mozambique established a National Reference Group for the SDGs (GRN-ODS) which has played a key role in coordinating the process and promoting the broad involvement of different sectors of government (central, provincial and district), civil society, academia, the private sector, cooperation partners, UN agencies, parliamentarians, the National Association of Municipalities of Mozambique (ANAMM); the African Peer Review Mechanism (MARP) and individuals. This reference group also played a decisive role in the 2020 Voluntary National Review process.

In order to ensure greater involvement of the different players, in addition to those described above, thematic groups were set up made up of representatives from the different Ministries, and CSOs (UN agencies, Association of Municipalities, International Cooperation Partners, academics and the private sector). The thematic groups were composed according to the following SDGs:

Social area: SDGs 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10 and 17 Economic Area: SDGs 7, 8, 9, 11 and 12 Mastering the environment: SDGs 13, 14 and 15 Governance: SDGs 16 and 17

These groups were coordinated by civil society organizations, namely in the economic area by the Mozambican Debt Group (GMD), the Foundation for Development (FDC) coordinated the social area, the Civil Society Platform for Social Protection (PSC-PS) was responsible for the area of governance and Development of Women and Girls (GDMR), and the UNDP coordinated the environmental area.

Also in this context, World Vision facilitated the consultation process with children. For an integrated analysis of the SDGs, each thematic group selected a central theme for the country’s priorities, which formed the focus of the discussion and allowed for an assessment of progress, challenges, and opportunities. The analysis focused on the process of implementing the 2030 Agenda, highlighting the indivisible nature of the SDGs and their close interrelationship, as well as the implementation mechanisms, which include partnerships and the means to do so.

Civil society and other development actors are partners in the process of implementing the SDGs, as well as in reporting and accountability in the Provincial Development Observatories (ODPs). At district level, the participation of development actors is ensured through the Local Councils.

The Provincial Assemblies have the challenge of ensuring that the SDG indicators and targets are reflected in their strategies, plans, and budgets.

UNDP support for localizing the SDGs in Mozambique

Specifically in partnership with AACID, UNDP Mozambique’s Decentralization Program has been directly supporting the Localization of the SDGs in Maputo and Cabo Delgado Provinces, and also in partnership with the EU in Sofala and Manica (in the context of the EU-funded project Decentralization for Development: D4D), and in partnership with Swiss Cooperation in Niassa and Nampula (Initiatives for Development: I4D, funded by Swiss Cooperation). The three projects are complementary, and all contribute to achieving the SDGs.

The project Contributing to the territorialization of the 2030 Agenda: An AACID-UNDP strategic partnership aims to pilot and support efforts to localize the SDGs in Cabo Delgado, through various advocacy, policy analysis and advisory activities related to the SDGs, involving provincial, district and municipal authorities, as well as other local development actors in the province. This work informs and strengthens efforts and capacities to localize the SDGs in other parts of Mozambique through a partnership that brings together UNDP, the Mozambican authorities (Ministry of Economy and Finance), AACID and the UNDP/ART initiative as key partners. The Ministry of Economy and Finance has, among other things, the mandate to support capacity needs in the areas of planning and finance at the decentralized levels of the Mozambican public administration and the state. It is therefore well placed to use the experience, lessons and material drawn from the project’s work in Cabo Delgado to inform the work of localizing the SDGs in other provinces of the country.

Also with AACID, the UNDP is implementing the project The regional perspective in the SDG localization processes: the Andalusian reflection for the territorialization of the SDGs. Its general aim is to contribute to the localization of the SDGs from the perspective of governments through a partnership between the Junta de Andalucía and the UNDP, with the specific aim of promoting the localization of the SDGs in Maputo Province.

The central axes on which this joint initiative between UNDP and AACID has been developed revolve around strengthening the localization of the SDGs (individual capacities and at institutional level) and establishing territorial governance mechanisms for the revision of strategic plans for the SDGs.

Through the training and capacity building component, the aim is to create an institutional strategy to promote integrated sustainable development that includes tools and methodologies to support localization (including an action plan). In this sense, the creation of SDG Provincial Reference Groups aims to strengthen the decentralization promoted by the Mozambican state, supporting Maputo province in improving coordination between the different decentralized and deconcentrated structures present in the territory.

UNDP has been supporting in coordination with MEF, MAEFP, international partners, as well as other UN Agencies, CSOS and the private sector in the localization of the SDGs, as well as in the decentralization process, especially after the last decentralization reform. Objectives of the Guidelines

To serve as a single, uniform reference framework for stakeholders involved in the drafting and implementation of provincial territorial strategies for sustainable development, including members of the councils of the Provincial State Representation Services in the Province and the Provincial Executive Council, municipal technical staff, deconcentrated state services, technical and financial partners, sectoral leaders, the private sector, as well as NGOs and other representatives of civil society.

Significantly consolidate the coordinated, bottom-up participatory planning approach at national and local level. This will allow the authorities and their partners to consider the real needs of the province, based on the programming of investments.

Ensure coherence between development initiatives at local level and national policies and concerns, as well as international principles, as set out in the National Development Strategy (ENDE) 2025-2044, the 2030 Agenda and the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

Strengthen the link between local priorities, national policies and international processes through multi-sectoral Provincial Reference Groups that lead an inclusive and participatory approach to strategic planning.

Consolidate the leadership of local authorities and promote the appropriation of instruments and tools for the localization of the SDGs and for strategic planning by the real protagonists of their own development: the citizens in their provinces.

III. Status of the SDGs in Mozambique

The latest report on the incidence of poverty, based on the Family Budget Survey (IOF) carried out in 2014 and 2015, shows that 46.1% of the population in Mozambique lives below the poverty line, with poverty being higher in rural areas (53.1%) and relatively lower in urban areas (40.7%).The provinces of Niassa (65.3%), Nampula (61.4%), Zambézia (60%), Inhambane (50.8%) and Cabo Delgado (50%) are the ones with the highest poverty rates in the country.

Although agriculture is the economic backbone of Mozambique, chronic malnutrition (the main indicator of which is stunting) and acute malnutrition (low weight for age, food insecurity and nutritional insecurity) remain critical. The baseline study by the Technical Secretariat for Food and Nutrition Security (SETSAN, 2013) indicates that in Mozambique the rate of chronic malnutrition in children up to the age of 5 is considered high, at 43%. High rates of malnutrition occur even in provinces with considerable food production, such as Tete and Nampula.

According to the Afrobarometer opinion poll, the perception of the quality of health services improved between 2015 and 2018 - the number of people who had contact with health services increased from 55% to 65.7% and the number of people who said that the government was managing basic health services properly increased from 42% to 55%. However, according to the RNV surveys, there are still challenges to overcome in the provision of services, such as the lack of medicines, the long distances to health units and the need to improve health care.

From 2015 to 2018, the country saw significant growth in the number of schools and enrollments from 18,906 to around 24,000 within the categories of public general and technical day schools and more than 6.5 million students enrolled (2015) to just over 7.8 million students (2018), of which 3.8 million were female. Mozambique is close to achieving parity between boys and girls, with girls now representing 48.2% of the enrolled student population. In addition, 93% of children start elementary school at the correct age of 6. The net enrolment rate for girls rose from 83.9% to 92.5% between 2017 and 2018.

14

In Mozambique, 83.3% of the legal frameworks (legislation) that promote, apply, and monitor gender equality under the SDG indicator with a focus on violence against women are in place. In February 2021, 42.4% of seats in parliament were held by women. However, there is still work to be done in the country to achieve gender equality. 52.9% of women aged between 20 and 24 were married or in a civil partnership before the age of 18.

In Mozambique, water supply and sanitation policies are coordinated through the Water and Sanitation Group (GAS). There is also a Multisectoral Sanitation Group that includes Civil Society Organizations, which plans and coordinates the implementation of sanitation actions and policies in an integrated manner. National statistics show that, from 2014 to 2018, the urban population with access to water fell from 85% to 78%, while the rural population fell from 52% to 45%. By 2020, it is estimated that 61.8% of households will have access to drinking water sources. Only 35% of the population lives in districts with improved water and sanitation coverage, and the districts in the provinces with the highest concentration of people living below the poverty line have the lowest levels of access to water and sanitation.

Mozambique is committed to achieving the goal of universal access to affordable and sustainable energy by 2030. The 2015-2019 PQG estimated that 55% of the population would have access to electricity by 2019, while the 2020-2024 PQG estimated 64% by

  1. 2024. Achieving these targets helps to relieve the pressure on the use of firewood and
coal, one of the main sources of energy used by the population for cooking, and lighting homes, especially in outlying and rural areas, which in some way contributes to the degradation of the country’s forests and environment. Access to electricity grew positively between 2007 and 2017, from around 10% to 24% in 2019. However, a further boost is still needed to achieve the goal of universal access by 2030.
15

Mozambique has set itself the target of an economic growth rate of 7 to 8% of GDP for the 2015-2019 period, maintaining the growth of the previous decade, partially supported by investments in agriculture, mining, Foreign Direct Investment flows and Foreign Development Aid. However, from 2016 onwards, Mozambique began to experience a slowdown in economic activity due to (i) the fall in the prices of the main raw materials on the international market, (ii) the depreciation of the Metical against the main currencies, (iii) the reduction in Foreign Direct Investment and (iv) the suspension of Direct Budget Support. The combination of these factors led to average economic growth of 4.4% in the period from 2015 to 2018. With the slowdown in the economy, the government’s role of equitable distribution has taken on renewed importance, since income inequality, according to regional patterns, has been increasing. In addition, this slowdown has significantly limited economic transformation, so the structure of the economy has not changed significantly.

Mozambique’s industrial policy and strategy advocates the participation of the local industrial sector in mega-projects and the exploitation of natural resources as a way of promoting the competitiveness of the national productive fabric. In addition, the country has been investing in the energy, transport, and logistics sectors, as well as promoting the certification of companies using the Made in Mozambique label, creating solid conditions for the protection of the sugar and cement industries. Given the country’s vulnerability to climate change, the National Climate Change Adaptation and Mitigation Strategy (2013-2025) directs all sectors to integrate climate change adaptation (CCA) and disaster risk reduction (DRR) measures into their planning and budgeting processes.

In Mozambique, social inequalities are still significant and the Gini index, which measures inequalities, is 45.6. Poverty not only affects women disproportionately, but also has regional patterns. In addition, there are also social groups that are more subject to poverty, such as children, the elderly, and People with Disabilities (PwD). Mozambique is among the countries with one of the highest levels of inequality in sub-Saharan Africa and the disparity between better-off households and the most disadvantaged is increasing, as are regional asymmetries.

16

According to the 2017 Census, 90% of dwellings are self-owned and, given the predominance of self-construction, an estimated 80% of dwellings. The country has been making efforts to help the family sphere, especially in resilient self-construction practices using local materials, the success of which depends on the coordination of partnerships between the central government, provincial authorities and municipalities, and partnerships with local actors, associations, and construction companies. One of the risks with the greatest impact in Mozambique is the climate, as food, droughts, strong winds and cyclones are a regular threat to cities and communities.

In the SDG Index produced by the UN Sustainable Development Solutions Network, Mozambique performs well because it has low levels of pollution, which may be associated with the reduced industrial park. Access to storage has reduced food losses along the production and supply chains and, in Mozambique, only 53% of farmers had a place to store their produce during the 2014/2015 agricultural season. To reverse the situation, the government has allocated 10% of total state budget expenditure to agriculture, as provided for in the Maputo Agreement, and objective criteria have also been defined for allocating the provision of essential services and infrastructure, including the improvement of production and post-harvest technologies. Although the country does not have systematic data, “isolated studies indicate that post-harvest losses vary between 15% and 30%”.

Mozambique’s contribution to climate change is not considered noteworthy, so it will reach the target by 2030, since the global benchmark is the level of CO2 emissions. The country’s low level of industrialization results in low levels of greenhouse gas emissions from the industry, transport, and energy sectors. In Mozambique, CO2 emissions per capita have increased in recent years, but are still low. This is a common pattern for most low-income economies in sub-Saharan Africa and the low levels of industrial development in these countries, resulting in low greenhouse gas emissions from industry, transport, and the energy sector.

Mozambique has more than 2,700 km of coastline with abundant and diverse marine life. This wealth represents an important source of food for the country’s population, a vital job market for around 400,000 people and a significant source of income for many families, although its direct contribution to GDP is relatively low (less than 2%). The marine area represents 43% of the national territory and 2.5% of the conservation areas, with a deficit of 7.5% to reach the target of “by 2030, conserving at least 10% of coastal and marine areas, in accordance with national and international legislation”.

17

Biodiversity represents the livelihood of more than 90% of the human population for food, health, and energy. In addition, biological diversity plays an important role in the global climate balance. It also has a high value in the national economy since ecosystems provide goods and services that contribute to stabilizing rural and urban economies on a national scale. One of Mozambique’s main assets is its forests, which cover 70% of its territory, half of which are productive in terms of timber. In 2015, it was estimated that the country had 41 million hectares of forest and in just three years it has lost 7.3% of its cover, leaving it with a forest area of 38 million acres, according to the inventory carried out in 2018. To curb deforestation, the country has set a target of 0.21% deforestation (SDG 15.2). However, the rate observed between 2003 and 2013 was almost four times higher, reaching 0.79%. As a result, in two years (2014-2016) Mozambique lost an area of forest similar to what it had lost in the previous 10 years.

The measures aimed at consolidating peace were highlighted by the recent constitutional changes made in 2018, as part of the Peace Agreement between the government and the largest opposition party - Renamo (Mozambican National Resistance), which laid the foundations for the new Decentralization framework in Mozambique. The previous framework was a combination of political and administrative decentralization. The first was materialized through the municipalities, which enjoy Administrative, Financial and Asset Autonomy and the direct election of their heads. The second was made up of the so-called Local Organs of State (Provinces, Districts, Administrative Posts, Localities and Villages), representatives of the Central State at Local Level, with only the Members of the Provincial Assembly being elected. The New Decentralization Model changed the mechanisms for electing the President of the Municipal Council and introduced the indirect election of the Provincial and District Executives, the latter only coming into force as soon as the conditions are in place. Thus, the new Decentralization framework maintains the existence of Local Authorities and extends the powers of provincial bodies.

IV. The context of fiscal decentralization in

Mozambique

The new legislative framework has introduced several structural changes to the fiscal resources that the Provincial Decentralized Governance Bodies (OGDPs) can count on. Law no. 16/2019, of September 24, on the Financial and Assets Regime of Provincial Decentralized Governance Bodies, establishes in its article 6 that the revenues programmed in the OGDP’s annual Plans and Budgets (PO) consist, in principle, of two items: own revenues and fiscal resources transferred by the central government. With regard to access to credit, article 13 of the law is quite strict: loans are limited to short-term commercial credits, which must be approved by the PA and must be repaid with own revenues during the fiscal year in which they were contracted.

Regarding own revenues, Law 16/2019 establishes in article 15 the three main categories of sources available. These are:

a) own revenue from licensing, concession and sale of services;

(b) revenue from the rental or sale of assets, or from donations or gifts;

c) any other revenue from specific legislation.

The provisions of article 15 imply that OGDPs do not have access to tax revenue, i.e. they do not have their own tax base. This is different from the municipalities’ right to tax, which is in accordance with Law no. 1/2008 of January 16, which defines the financial, and patrimonial regime of local authorities, and the Municipal Tax System and the respective Municipal Tax Code approved by Decree 63/2008 of December 30. Thus, the resources at its disposal depend mainly on transfers from the central administration and non-tax revenue, i.e. user fees and licenses. The collection of non-tax revenue is shared between various actors, such as the central government, OGDP, districts, municipalities, etc., and the boundaries of both competencies and limits are unclear.

Licensing fees for the provision of services cannot be considered sources of tax revenue in the strict sense of the term, nor regular revenue, because they are generated from a service to be provided or a license to be granted at the request of a citizen. This means that their own revenue can hardly be predicted. In other words, the new decentralized provincial bodies will depend mainly on government transfers. This reinforces their dependence on the central government and weakens the notion of autonomy.5

Since their own resource base is limited to non-tax revenue, one of the challenges and priorities for all the LDPGs is to calculate their own revenue base and have a functioning revenue collection apparatus, for which technical assistance is needed. However, given the effects of the global economic crises, own revenue collection might not thrive and might not be enough to provide them with a solid tax base. This creates a high-risk situation for the OGDP, with their limited status of “exceptional autonomy”, which the new Law No. 14/2020 of December 236 , known as the SISTAFE Law, gives them.

Preliminary methodological note

Plan/Planning Planning is the process of programming actions or interventions in time and space, together with the corresponding resources. Interventions must be coordinated with each other to achieve a harmonious whole. It includes a set of methods and strategies to ensure the implementation of development actions.

A plan is a reference document that includes a set of objectives and priorities, strategies and actions, as well as identifying the resources needed to implement them. A plan clarifies for its recipients the options available and guides the players on how to achieve the development objectives.

Programming/Program Programming is the distribution of a set of actions, activities or interventions needed to achieve an objective, or a set of objectives, in a given period. It must combine the logic of the plan, the expected results, the planned activities and the available resources.

A program is an organized set of policies, procedures and projects, operations and services designed for one or more common objectives.

Priority/Prioritization Priorities are a set of actions ordered in a hierarchy according to a set of predetermined criteria, such as the relevance of a problem, the urgency of taking action or the availability of resources to implement them.

Prioritization is the ranking or programming of actions in order of importance, according to the seriousness or weight of the problems to be solved and the available, necessary or negotiable resources.

Resources A set of human, material and financial resources to carry out the planned activities and necessary to obtain the expected results.

Control/Evaluation Monitoring is the permanent or periodic review of the implementation of an action program.

Evaluation is an analysis of the effects and impacts of a program. It is based on a comparison between the expected results and those that have been achieved.

Step 5 “Leave No One Behind” (LNOB) study at

In promoting inclusive and transparent processes in the localization of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the commitment to “Leave No One Behind” (LNOB) must be consistently upheld. The LNOB represents the central, transformative promise of the 2030 Agenda, symbolizing the unequivocal commitment of all UN Member States to eradicate poverty in all its forms, eliminate discrimination and exclusion, and reduce the inequalities and vulnerabilities that marginalize individuals and undermine the potential of humanity as a whole.

When analyzing the situation of the “Leave No One Behind” principle in a province, it is essential to consider the following relevant issues:

Who? Identify the main groups being left behind or at risk of being left behind in Mozambique. Why? Determine the immediate and root/structural causes that increase the risk of falling behind. What? Identify the measures that should be taken to ensure that no one is left behind and that the risks of being left behind are mitigated. What is the geographical location and human rights perspective of the people identified as being left behind?

To understand the reasons why people are being left behind, it is essential to consider the five factors that often contribute to vulnerability: Discrimination; Geography; Governance; Socio-economic Status; and Vulnerability to Shocks.

33

Mozambique has made significant progress in its efforts to leave no one behind. Over the last 20 years, maternal and infant mortality rates have been reduced and there has been a substantial improvement in access to basic education, water and electricity. The combination of these achievements is reflected in the improvement in the life expectancy of Mozambicans from 49.4 years in 2007 to 53.7 in 2019.

According to the Overseas Development Institute’s (ODI) Leave No One Behind Index (2020), which measures countries’ preparedness for the LNOB, Mozambique is “partially prepared” in terms of political data (equal access to employment, health and land). However, it also indicates that the country is “not ready” in terms of finances (education, health and social protection). The LNOB’s overall readiness index highlights that Mozambique is “partially ready” but “off track” in improving SDG outcomes (under-five mortality rate, malnutrition, access to finance and electricity). Efforts are therefore needed to ensure that Mozambique is better equipped to leave no one behind (Chattopadhyay & Manea 2019).

A significant part of the Mozambican population continues to face exclusion due to historical or temporary and intersecting forms of vulnerability, as well as institutional and capacity gaps that prevent them from asserting their rights and fulfilling their duties, respectively. The UN Common Country Analysis (2021) reveals that there are approximately 22 categories of vulnerable groups in Mozambique. Variables such as gender, age, location, economic situation, gender identity and migration status are factors that can increase the risk of exclusion. It can be seen, for example, that women and girls; children and young people; the elderly; people with disabilities; and people in situations of forced displacement face exacerbated challenges. In addition, due to structural gender-based discrimination, which starts at a young age, women are at a disproportionate risk of being left behind.

Consequently, the groups identified face barriers to the full enjoyment of human rights, access to basic services and opportunities to develop their full potential and participate in the country’s development path. For this reason, the UNDP in Mozambique carried out a study in six provinces delimited on the basis of the unique methodological tool for implementing the LNOB.

By focusing on LNOB, the methodology aims to contribute to both the decentralization and localization processes of the SDGs in Mozambique, offering a unique opportunity for provincial governments to orient themselves in relation to strategic planning, programs and projects with a multi-actor approach, working with vulnerable groups to design customized projects and solutions based on their specific context, to cover their specific needs.

The main result of implementing the methodology is the production of a Leaving No One Behind (LNOB) Diagnostic Report for each Province, which provides a comprehensive analysis of the situation in the province. This report not only identifies who is being left behind, but also highlights those who are most disadvantaged. In addition, the study will provide provincial governments with a clear vision of whether the needs of vulnerable groups in their respective provinces are reflected in the public policies, projects and local programs currently in place. Finally, the diagnosis includes a series of recommendations on how to support the groups that are being left behind.

The Diagnosis serves as a framework for provinces to decide how to design their Provincial Territorial Strategies in order to meet the needs of vulnerable groups within the province, ultimately working to ensure that no one is left behind. The LNOB approach within the Provincial Territorial Strategies will support the process of aligning provincial plans with the SDGs. In this way, the Diagnostic Report serves to enrich the process of localizing the SDGs within the provinces.

Step 10 Turning Ideas into Action:

On December 11 and 12, 2023, Eduardo Mondlane University (UEM) held an International Congress that highlighted the fundamental role of universities in integrating the 2030 Agenda. This event brought together higher education institutions from Mozambique and Andalusia, providing a valuable space for exchanging experiences on mainstreaming the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). During the sessions, representatives from UNDP, the Ministry of Economy and Finance and AACID emphasized the importance of collaboration between universities and other players in implementing the SDGs, highlighting innovative initiatives and the need for effective partnerships. The exchange of knowledge and practices between universities not only enriched the debate, but also strengthened the alliances needed to meet the challenges of sustainable development in Mozambique.

As a result of the strategic partnership between the Ministry of Economy and Finance (MEF), UNDP, Eduardo Mondlane University (UEM) and the University of Córdoba (Spain), a reference document will be produced that systematizes the experience of supporting the process of localizing the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) at provincial level in Mozambique. This document will describe in a narrative and didactic way the process of conceiving, designing and implementing the localization of the SDGs in the provinces of Cabo Delgado and Maputo, focusing on the lessons learned, the involvement of the actors, the challenges and solutions encountered, and the alignment with national and local processes. This joint publication between universities in Mozambique and Spain, in partnership with the government, will serve as a valuable resource to guide future SDG localization efforts, sharing best practices and lessons learned from this pioneering process.

Step 17 Impact project

The final stage of the localization process consists of implementing projects designed to improve the living conditions of people, the province, the country and the planet. The process of selecting projects related to the SDGs is participatory and conducted by the Provincial Reference Group itself.

To this end, the UNDP is making financial resources available so that each province can choose a priority project linked to the SDGs, which forms part of the Provincial Territorial Strategy.

The aim of this localization exercise is for all international cooperation actors wishing to support the development of the provinces to use these strategic plans, promoting harmonization with the state’s planning instruments and allowing for the evaluation of progress and investments made in the SDGs at a local level.

Each province will have to select an impact project in a participatory way; The project must prioritize the location of the SDGs; The project must be aligned with the provincial, district and municipal strategy; The project must ensure the involvement of various stakeholders in coordination and partnerships; People must be the central focus of these projects.

Example in Niassa Province of the selection of the SDG Impact Project:

In Niassa Province, during the Thematic Commissions, the three stages mentioned in the figure above were implemented for the selection of the SDG Impact Project. The impact project selection meeting was attended by around 92 participants, 60 male and 32 female, representing the CEP, CSREP, Districts, Municipalities and Civil Society institutions. Thus, during a 3-day event, the first day was dedicated to the first phase - Training and Methodology. The UNDP presented the methodology for presenting the Impact project. With the aim of providing all the information needed to formulate and present projects, the thematic areas, the value and duration of the project, the structuring criteria, the cross-cutting areas, the administrative management of projects and the steps to be taken from the idea to the approval of the project were shared. The presentation also covered the criteria for purchasing goods and services, eligible and ineligible expenses and, finally, changes to the project that may or may not have to be notified to the UNDP. The prioritization of activities was also discussed.

Another moment was dedicated to a practical review of project preparation. Considering possible disparity in knowledge between the participants, a methodology based on a mixed approach was used, between sharing content related to project planning, practical exercises and plenary presentations. In this session, participants reinforced their knowledge of identifying and justifying the problem, defining objectives, goals and target groups, defining the methodology and formulating indicators (SMART), identifying partners allied to the project, budgeting, drawing up a timetable and mapping risks.

The first day ended with a reflection on Gender Equality, which included an approach to planning from a gender perspective, led by the Forum of Women’s Organizations of Niassa (FOFeN).

The second day was dedicated to identifying projects, along with a review of the sectoral pillars. With the aim of making participants aware of the current state of the different sectors (health, education, social action, infrastructure, agriculture and the environment), the challenges of the sectors and the opportunities for developing projects in response to the needs identified were presented and debated. The thematic groups then reviewed the province’s project portfolio to align it with ENDE and the SDGs. In the same exercise, each group received an average of 7 projects to locate and prioritize according to the SDGs and ENDE. Following this exercise, each group had the opportunity to share the results of the exercise.

The third and final day was dedicated to defining priorities and project selection. Participants worked in thematic groups to identify project ideas. To this end, the ENDE pillars and their objectives were shared, as well as the project form.

This session had a mixed orientation, UNDP and MEF. From the exercise, the committees proposed some relevant projects.

After the project ideas were presented and analyzed in plenary, there was a deviation in the group’s objectives, the omission of which was the identification of projects for the Governance pillar and transformation of the Structure of the Economy. However, based on the presentations and justifications given, the participants came up with three ideas: Building a maternity hospital with a waiting room for pregnant women and water boreholes in different districts. In conclusion, the participants were unanimous in approving an integrated project to be implemented in a single location so that the impact could be better visualized. The village of Lizombe, in the district of Majune, was chosen to host the project to build a maternity hospital with 3 beds, a waiting house and a water borehole for the communities.

Conclusions

The main conclusions of the localization process supported by six provinces in Mozambique were as follows:

  1. 1. The importance of aligning the SDG localization process with existing strategic planning mechanisms to ensure the sustainability of the process.

  1. 2. Propose innovative tools that allow us to better understand the territory in order to make strategic decisions - an example of the LNOB case.

  1. 3. The localization of the SDGs must involve all the actors present in the territory. The whole process must be inclusive and participatory, sometimes using existing dialogue mechanisms
such as development observatories.

  1. 4. There was a need for a provincial technical group to coordinate the process and draw up the

  1. 5. Communication campaigns adapted to the local population are needed. The language of the SDGs can sometimes be very complex; it is therefore necessary to translate the messages
into local languages and adapt them to each local context. In this way, each citizen will better understand their relevant role in achieving the SDGs.

  1. 6. Existing mechanisms need to be used in line with national mechanisms. In the case of Mozambique, the government created the multi-sectoral National Reference Group, so at a
local level and to ensure alignment, provincial reference groups were created in each province.

  1. 7. National and local ownership is essential. Although the UNDP provided methodological and technical support to the Reference Groups in carrying out their work, the localization process
was entirely owned by the Ministry of Economy and Finance and each province. This has ensured the existence of a group made up of national and local experts, who can communicate the methodology to the provinces.
